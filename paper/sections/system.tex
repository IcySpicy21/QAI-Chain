\section{System Design}

The runtime story is simple to draw even if the implementation is not: telemetry in, policy proposes something, an uncertainty gate may veto it, a shield always gets the last word, then the actuators see a bounded action. Blockchain shows up after the fact as an append-only receipt if you want multi-party review.

\[
\text{state telemetry} \rightarrow \text{policy proposal} \rightarrow \text{uncertainty gate} \rightarrow \text{shield} \rightarrow \text{executed action}.
\]

Commits land on chain once an action is finalized so the adaptive bits stay in Python/Sim and the ledger stays a witness, not a hot path for gradient math.

\subsection{Data and Control Flow}

RPC and validation code advance ledger state from peer and transaction events. The controller reads a compressed governance vector and emits bounded knobs (difficulty, block size, gas limit). The diagram is the same idea as above, just drawn with the shield spelled out:

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\text{State telemetry} &\rightarrow \text{policy proposal} \rightarrow \text{uncertainty gate} \\
&\rightarrow \text{safety shield} \rightarrow \text{on-chain commit}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

The gate may punt to a heuristic before execution; the shield may clamp or reject. Numbers in the paper all assume that gated-and-shielded path. Appendix material carries the extra protocol detail if you care about receipts and Merkle layouts.

\subsection{API Contracts}

RPC payloads are typed; garbage-in gets a hard error. We keep negative-path tests around malformed fields so the adaptive layer cannot silently widen the contract surface.

\subsection{Operational Guardrails}

Typed interfaces, those RPC tests, and a deterministic ordering between policy, gate, and shield are what keep multi-node runs from drifting into ``works on my laptop'' territory. None of that is novel science, but it is what makes the behavior legible when something breaks at 2\,am.
